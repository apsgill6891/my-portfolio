Navigation Principles

How I think about complex problems

These aren't abstract values — each one was learned in a specific moment, on a ship or in a boardroom, where the wrong call would have cost lives, capital, or trust.

Read the signals early
Sailors watched birds and cloud formations to anticipate storms hours before they hit. At Cascade Aerospace, when efficiency gains had plateaued, I built a new Gantt visualisation of stage-by-stage data and discovered we were running 6 concurrent aircraft when the contract specified 4 — a hidden contention nobody had named. Turnaround time dropped from 180 to 105 days.
"Red sky at night, sailor's delight. Red sky in morning, sailor's warning."
Find your North Star
Strategy only works when every decision traces back to a clear, long-term direction. At OLG, I kept eight executives aligned during a contentious strategy workshop by anchoring every trade-off to the single question: what moves us toward FY27 targets? Consensus on integrating Operational Efficiency into four other Strategic Priorities followed.
"He who knows not his destination will find no wind favourable."
Measure before acting
Navigators used sextants and star charts because intuition alone is not enough on open water. At Delhivery, I mastered AutoCAD to personally evaluate 200+ facility drawings — then automated vehicle clearance checks from 15 minutes to 1 minute per drawing, eliminating subjective debate and reducing errors to zero.
"A navigator who doesn't check his charts is gambling with his crew."
Stay steady in uncertainty
When a ship's engine failed mid-ocean, five cross-functional teams needed coordination simultaneously — propulsion, communications, weather monitoring, coast guard contact, and passenger safety. Panic would have compounded the crisis. Calm methodology contained it. I've carried this into boardrooms: when Blowhorn faced a 30% salary cut, I prioritised honest, upfront communication over false reassurance.
"Smooth seas do not make skilful sailors."
Respect the system
Oceans, like organisations, have currents you cannot see from the surface. At Blowhorn, the real problem wasn't driver performance — it was that the business model had 65% structural failure baked in through ad-hoc supply sourcing. The fix wasn't a performance plan; it was a dedicated Supply Transformation Team and a codified playbook. Failure rate: 65% → 5%.
"The sea is the same sea as always, but the ships and the men have changed."
Look beyond the horizon
Curiosity is the navigator's greatest tool. Every industry I've entered — maritime, logistics, aerospace, strategy — required rapid new skills from scratch. AutoCAD in 30 days. Aerospace maintenance operations in months. Today I'm exploring AI systems, having built an agent prototype that saved CAD 165k/year for a manufacturing SMB. The willingness to learn is what keeps you relevant in uncharted waters.
"Not all those who wander are lost."

Aerospace Operations $270M Contract
Finding the Hidden Bottleneck in a Failing Aerospace Program
The USAF Avionics upgrade program at Cascade Aerospace had stalled. Turnaround time was 180 days against a 120-day target, improvement efforts had plateaued, and a $270M NATO contract renewal hung in the balance. Previous analyses had looked at individual technician productivity, parts availability, and training gaps — all real issues, but none explained the sustained plateau. I built relationships with initially sceptical stakeholders on the shop floor and in engineering, collected granular stage-by-stage data (non-routines, training events, aircraft robbing), and re-visualised it as a Gantt chart. The pattern was immediately clear: we were routinely handling 6 aircraft simultaneously when the contract specified 4. The contention for shared resources — equipment, certified technicians, inspection bays — was the hidden constraint nobody had named.
The key insight
Concurrency was the constraint, not individual efficiency. Running 6 aircraft simultaneously created resource contention that no amount of technician coaching could solve.
The difficult decision
Convinced the COO to reduce concurrent aircraft from 6 to 4 — a short-term throughput sacrifice for long-term TAT improvement. This required data-backed persuasion against strong operational pressure.

Maritime Crisis Leadership Annual Safety Award
Averting $50M in Damages During a Mid-Ocean Engine Failure
Serving as Marine Navigating Officer with MOL on a vessel carrying 5,000 cars across the Pacific, the ship suffered a complete engine failure in open water. In the maritime industry, a dead ship in adverse weather conditions can quickly become a total loss. The situation required simultaneous coordination across five teams — propulsion engineering, bridge navigation, communications, weather monitoring, and coast guard liaison — all under time pressure, with no room for ambiguity in instructions. I led the cross-functional response: directed troubleshooting communications between engineering and the bridge, maintained weather data monitoring to track drift, coordinated distress communications, and kept the crew calm through clear, decisive updates. The engine was restored before the vessel drifted into hazardous proximity to land.
The key insight
In a crisis, the quality of coordination matters more than the speed of any individual action. Structured, clear communication across five simultaneous teams prevented compounding errors.
The lasting lesson
Calm is a leadership strategy. Every organisation I've joined since has benefited from this: steady judgment under pressure is rarer and more valuable than technical expertise alone.

Logistics Infrastructure $135M Revenue Synergy
Scaling India's Largest Logistics Infrastructure — 150+ Projects Simultaneously
Delhivery had acquired SpotOn and needed to integrate 150+ facilities across India rapidly — greenfield builds, brownfield retrofits, and capacity expansions — while maintaining operational continuity. The infrastructure team I led had one critical bottleneck: AutoCAD drawing approvals were slow and subjective, causing delays that cascaded into construction timelines. I had never used AutoCAD before joining the role. Within one month I was approving 20,000 sq ft facilities. Within four months, I had cleared 200+ facilities ranging from 10k to 300k sq ft. I then automated vehicle clearance checks from a 15-minute manual process to a 1-minute automated simulation — eliminating both the delay and the recurring debates between teams.
The key insight
The approval bottleneck wasn't a capacity problem — it was an objectivity problem. Automating the truck simulation check removed subjectivity entirely and made every decision auditable.
The self-directed learning
Mastered AutoCAD from scratch using old drawings, observation, and after-hours practice — a "learning by doing" approach that I've since applied to aerospace operations and AI systems.

Logistics Operations Design 65% → 5% Failure Rate
Turning a Failing Supply Model into a Scalable National Network
Blowhorn's last-mile logistics operation had a 65% trip failure rate — meaning 2 in every 3 trips either didn't complete or completed incorrectly. The root cause was structural: supply sourcing was entirely ad-hoc, reliant on personal relationships, with no standardised pricing, verification, or accountability. During a peak holiday season demand surge, this became untenable. Rather than applying local fixes, I convinced the Head of Operations to form a dedicated Supply Transformation Team — a strategic resource allocation rather than a tactical patch. I divided the city into 7 regions, hired 7 dedicated supply managers, and built a Supply Playbook codifying tariffs, taxes, verification processes, and driver onboarding. The CEO then deployed me to replicate this in 20+ additional cities across India.
The key insight
Personal relationships don't scale. The failure rate wasn't a people problem — it was a systems absence. The fix was to build the system that should have existed from day one.
The persuasion challenge
Convincing leadership to invest in a dedicated supply team during a crisis required data-backed projections and the discipline to argue for a structural change when the pressure was for immediate action.

Strategy Consulting Executive Alignment CAD $25M Projected Revenue
Building Executive Consensus for a $25M Lottery Partnership Strategy
At the Ontario Lottery and Gaming Corporation (revenue ~CAD 4.7B), I was tasked with two parallel challenges: develop a novel partnership strategy for lottery terminal expansion, and co-facilitate a strategy workshop where eight executives needed to reach consensus on FY27 strategic priorities. For the partnership strategy, I modelled the initiative on India's Lucky Yatra program — where train ticket numbers double as lottery entries — and translated the mechanism to the Canadian context: a partnership that leveraged existing customer touchpoints to drive lottery engagement. This secured CEO buy-in for a pilot of 25 lottery terminals with projected revenues of CAD ~$25M. In the parallel workshop, I co-facilitated Visual Prioritisation and Orthodoxy-Flip exercises to surface hidden assumptions and guide eight executives — with competing departmental interests — toward a unified view on integrating Operational Efficiency as a shared focus across four other Strategic Priorities.
The strategy insight
The best innovation doesn't always require invention — it requires translation. Recognising that a proven mechanism from a different market (India's Lucky Yatra) was directly applicable to OLG's context unlocked a new growth avenue.
The facilitation method
Executive alignment requires structured divergence before convergence. Orthodoxy-Flip exercises forced executives to articulate the assumptions behind their positions, making trade-offs visible and depersonalising disagreement.
