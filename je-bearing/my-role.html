First-Person Contributions

What I did, specifically

🎯

Problem Framing

I reframed the shop scheduling challenge from a pure scheduling optimization problem into a decision-support problem. This distinction matters enormously. A scheduling optimizer would try to produce an optimal schedule. A decision-support system produces visible trade-offs that a human expert can evaluate with full context.

I identified that the weekly planning meeting — not the ERP system — was the shop's real optimization engine. The work was to support that meeting with better information, not to automate it away. This framing shaped every downstream design decision.

🗄️

Canonical Data Modeling (Module 0)

I designed the canonical data structure for all six core entity types: operators, machines, jobs, routings, parameters, and constraints. For each entity, I defined which attributes were necessary for decision support (not just data recording), what the canonical completeness criteria were, and how the entities related to each other.

This included translating verbal rules — "Joe prefers VMC1 because it holds tighter tolerances" — into explicit, queryable fields like preferredMachines, precisionClass, and certifiedMachines.

📊

Weekly Review Logic (Module 1)

I built the weekly review logic around real human constraints — not theoretical optimization. The job risk scoring formula, operator load scoring, and scenario generation logic were all designed to mirror how an experienced planner actually thinks: "What must ship? Who's overloaded? Where can I save a setup?"

I designed four scenarios (Min Setups, Min Inventory, Max On-Time, Operator-Friendly) to reflect the actual strategic trade-offs that arise in a HMLV shop week-to-week. Each scenario is not just a label — it has specific optimization biases that produce meaningfully different outputs.

The decision flags panel was designed to surface actionable recommendations, not just data. The system should tell the planner what decision to make, not just what the data says.

🔍

Same/Similar Part Intelligence

I developed the same/similar part intelligence concept as a two-layer system: structured ERP attribute matching, and geometry/image-assisted similarity. The structured layer uses a weighted similarity formula across material, setup family, routing family, machine family, tolerance, geometry, and run history. I defined the weightings based on which attributes most strongly predict setup reusability.

The geometry layer is a product concept for how a Gemini Vision or fine-tuned classifier could eventually classify part images and extend the matching to shops without well-structured ERP data. I designed the workflow and user experience — including the mock output format, confidence bands, and savings estimation logic.

🎨

Interface and Portfolio Design

I designed the interface logic for how users would interact with the system — what controls matter, what outputs to surface, what the information hierarchy should be. The goal was not a generic dashboard, but a product-grade tool that feels purposeful and credible to an operations audience.

This portfolio site is itself a design artifact — it demonstrates not just what I built but how I think about presenting technical work to a non-technical business audience.
